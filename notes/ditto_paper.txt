                                             Abstract

           Humans learn social norms and behaviors from verbal feedback (e.g., a
           parent saying “that was rude” or a friend explaining “here’s why that hurt”).
           Yet, learning from feedback for LLMs has largely focused on domains like
           code and math where RL rewards are directly verifiable and condensed
           into scalar values. As LLMs are increasingly also used for simulating
           human behavior, e.g., standing in for users, patients, students, and other
           personas, there is a pressing need to make them more human-like, which
           requires embracing a fundamentally different kind of signal: feedback that
           is verbal, subjective, and multi-faceted. We present D ITTO, a model trained
           by treating verbal feedback as a first-class signal in reinforcement learning.
           After each rollout, D ITTO receives verbal feedback and generates a feedback-
           conditioned improved rollout; both outputs are jointly optimized with
           GRPO, distilling verbal guidance into the base policy without requiring
           feedback at test time. We also introduce S OUL (Simulation gym Of hUman-
           Like behavior), a unified benchmark and training data suite spanning
           10 tasks across six categories: Theory of Mind, character role play, social
           skill, learner simulation, user simulation, and persona simulation. D ITTO
           achieves an average 36% improvement over the base model and exceeds
           GPT-5.4 on 6 of 10 S OUL benchmarks, demonstrating that RL with verbal
           feedback is a promising direction for training LLMs to simulate human
           behavior.


1     Introduction

Modeling human behavior has long been recognized as a crucial step toward imbuing
artificial intelligence with social intelligence (Tomasello, 2019; Nass & Moon, 2000). From the
Turing test to modern user simulators, the drive to faithfully model how people think, speak,
and act has evolved into a practical necessity for training and evaluating collaborative AI
systems. Today, LLMs are increasingly called upon to serve as these vital proxies—standing
in for users in dialogue systems (Davidson et al., 2023; Sekulic et al., 2024), for patients
in clinical settings (Schmidgall et al., 2024), for students in educational platforms (Ross &
Andreas, 2025; Dou et al., 2025), and for diverse personas in social and safety testing (Zhou
et al., 2024; 2025; Wang et al., 2025).
Yet current LLM simulators fall short, exhibiting systematic Sim2Real gaps—from homoge-
neous outputs lacking individual variation (Jiang et al., 2025), to misaligned cognitive biases
(Tjuatja et al., 2024), superhuman behaviors (Oh & Linzen, 2025), and consistent divergences
from real users in downstream tasks (Zhou et al., 2026b). One reason for this gap may lie in
how these models are trained. Reinforcement learning for LLMs, whether RLHF (Ouyang
et al., 2022), DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), or RL with verifiable rewards (DeepSeek-AI, 2025;

Shao et al., 2024), reduces all feedback to a scalar reward or binary preference. This suffices
when correctness is verifiable: a proof is right or wrong, a test passes or fails. But simulating
human behavior is not about solving a puzzle correctly; it is about acting in ways that
are socially grounded, individually varied, and contextually appropriate. A score of 0.6
conveys almost nothing about why a simulated user was too polite or too predictable, let
alone how to fix it. Humans themselves learn social behavior not from scores but from
verbal feedback, through explanations, corrections, and dialogue (Tomasello, 2019), and we
argue that training human simulators should follow a similar principle.
As shown in Figure 2, we present D ITTO, a model that incorporates verbal feedback as
a first-class signal in reinforcement learning for human behavior simulation (Song et al.,
2026; Shi et al., 2026). After each rollout, D ITTO receives verbal feedback and generates an
improved rollout conditioned on it; both outputs are jointly optimized via GRPO, so that the
base policy internalizes the verbal guidance without requiring it at test time (§3). Crucially,
our approach is flexible across task formats: it handles discriminative tasks (multiple-choice,
binary classification), generative tasks (open-ended dialogue), and multi-turn interactions
where the model must sustain human-like behavior across an entire conversation, all within
a single unified training framework.
To train and evaluate D ITTO, we introduce S OUL (Simulation gym Of hUman-Like be-
havior; Figure 3), a unified benchmark and training data suite comprising 10 tasks across
six categories of human behavior modeling : theory of mind (Kim et al., 2023; He et al.,
2024; Jin et al., 2024; Nematzadeh et al., 2018; Sclar et al., 2023), character role play (Wang
et al., 2026), social skill (Zhou et al., 2024), learner simulation (Ross & Andreas, 2025),
user simulation (Hathidara et al., 2026; Naous et al., 2025), and persona simulation (Du
et al., 2025). A key bottleneck for RL-based human simulation has been the absence of
training data: most existing human simulation benchmarks provide only evaluation sets,
not training splits. S OUL addresses this by pairing every task with curated RL training data,
adapting official splits where available and constructing new ones where not, such as 6,000
synthetic belief-tracking scenarios for HiToM, and 1,358 persona items for TwinVoice built
from three external corpora via a two-stage LLM generation and filtering pipeline. The
resulting mixture spans discriminative, generative, multi-turn, and multi-agent formats
within a single unified RL training framework (§4).
Our experiments on S OUL yield several findings. First, our Qwen3-8B D ITTO model achieves
an average normalized score of 0.726, a 36% improvement over the base model, while
matching or exceeding GPT-5.4 on 6 of 10 tasks. Gains are broad: D ITTO outperforms
GPT-5.4 on all three Theory of Mind tasks, on both user simulation tasks (e.g., scoring 0.930
compared to GPT-5.4’s 0.575 on UserLLM), and on Sotopia social skill (0.470 vs. 0.300),
spanning discriminative, generative, and multi-turn formats. Second, verbal feedback

2     Related Work

2.1    Incorporating Verbal Feedback in RL Training

Standard RL for language models—whether RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), DPO (Rafailov
et al., 2023), or GRPO (DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Shao et al., 2024)—reduces all feedback to
scalar rewards or binary preferences. To handle complex feedback signals, another line of
work decomposes the scalar reward into finer-grained or multi-dimensional signals while
remaining in the scalar regime (Wu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). These
methods enrich the reward structure but still compress each dimension into a scalar, losing
rich nature of verbal feedback.
To incorporate verbal feedback into improving LLM agents, prior work has explored su-
pervised fine-tuning (Scheurer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) or inference-time self-reflection
(Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023), but these approaches lack the exploration capacity
of RL or do not update model weights from experience. A recent line of work instead incor-
porates verbal feedback directly into RL optimization. Self-Rewarding Language Models
(Yuan et al., 2024) generate verbal self-judgments to construct preference pairs for iterative
DPO. Experiential RL (Shi et al., 2026) introduces an experience-reflection-consolidation
loop mixed RL and supervised fine-tuning. Concurrent to our approach, RLTF (Song et al.,
2026) formalizes reinforcement learning from text feedback and proposes self-distillation
(RLTF-SD), which trains the single-turn policy to match its own feedback-conditioned
second-turn generations. However, these works focus extensively on domains with veri-
fiable rewards, such as mathematics and reasoning puzzles. Human behavior simulation
poses fundamentally different challenges: interactions are multi-turn, rewards are subjective
and multi-dimensional.

2.2    Simulating Human Behavior with LLMs

LLM-based human simulation spans a broad landscape of tasks. User simulation has
evolved from rule-based and statistical approaches for dialogue management (Schatzmann
et al., 2006; Li et al., 2016) to LLM-based simulators for task-oriented dialogue (Davidson
et al., 2023; Sekulic et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2022), information-seeking (Sun et al., 2021; Zhang
et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024), and recommendation (Wang et al., 2025; Bougie & Watanabe,
2025). Beyond user simulation, LLMs are increasingly used for social interaction (Zhou
et al., 2024; 2025), character role-play (Wang et al., 2026), persona simulation (Samuel et al.,
2025), and learner simulation (Ross & Andreas, 2025).

However, recent efforts have revealed systematic Sim2Real gaps: LLM-simulated human

with multi-dimensional rewards. However, each targets a narrow set of tasks (e.g., Yu et al.

work incorporates verbal feedback into RL to improve a single model across the 10 diverse
tasks in S OUL, spanning six categories of human behavior modeling.


3     Method

3.1    Problem Formulation

We study the problem of training a language model for human simulation tasks. Given an
input x (e.g., an instruction, scenario, dialogue history, or user profile), the model generates
an output trajectory
                                  y = (y1 , . . . , y T ) ∼ πθ (· | x ),
where πθ is the current policy.
Each sampled trajectory is evaluated by a task-specific judge or simulator, which returns
supervision for learning. In the standard RL setting, this supervision is reduced to a scalar
reward
                                        r ( x, y) ∈ R.
Our goal is to train the policy πθ to maximize task performance in these human simulation
tasks, while allowing training to exploit richer judge supervision when available.

3.2    Verbal Feedback

In many human simulation tasks, the judge provides not only a scalar reward but also
rich verbal feedback, such as dimension-wise scores, textual critiques, and task-specific
suggestions. Formally, for a rollout y ∼ πθ (· | x ), the judge returns
                                       (r, h) = J ( x, y),                                              (1)

where r is a scalar reward and h is structured feedback describing what was done well, what
went wrong, and how the behavior can be improved.
This feedback is often highly informative. For example, in human behavior simulation, it
may reflect dimensions such as believability, relationship maintenance, knowledge consis-
tency, goal achievement, and adherence to social norms. Standard RL methods typically
compress all such information into a single scalar reward, which can lose useful learning
signal.
To ensure robustness of learning, we cast h as privileged information (Vapnik & Vashist,
2009; Vapnik & Izmailov, 2015): it is available during training but not at test time. Our goal
is therefore to use this verbal feedback to improve the base policy πθ (· | x ), while keeping
inference-time generation unchanged.

3.3   Learning from Verbal Feedback

We aim to optimize the base policy πθ (y | x ) using reward and feedback during training,
so the policy internalizes feedback-driven improvements without requiring feedback at
inference time.
To achieve this, for each sampled rollout
                                                y0 ∼ πθ (· | x ),                                      (2)
we ask the judge to provide reward and feedback
                                            (r0 , h) = J ( x, y0 ),                                    (3)
and then sample a second rollout conditioned on the same feedback:
                                   y1 ∼ πθ (· | x, h),       r1 = R( x, y1 ).                          (4)
Here, y0 is the original rollout from the base policy, which we refer to as the draft rollout,
while y1 is the feedback-conditioned rollout, which we refer to as the refined rollout. Both
rollouts are generated by the same policy with shared model weights; they differ only in
their prompting context, where the refined rollout additionally uses explicit verbal feedback
to guide improvement.
We optimize both behaviors during training. First, we train the base policy to learn from
feedback-induced improvements without conditioning on feedback at inference. Second,
we include an additional GRPO objective on feedback-conditioned rollouts themselves, so
the model also becomes better at generating improved responses from feedback during
training.

3.4   Connections to Other RL Approaches

Our approach is inspired by, or developed concurrently with, several recent works on RL
with textual feedback. We discuss the most relevant connections below.

Connection to RLTF Our method is related to RL from Text Feedback (RLTF; Song et al.,
2026), which also uses training-time text feedback to improve single-turn performance.
Both methods first generate a student output, then a feedback-conditioned teacher output,
and train the policy to absorb the teacher’s improvement. Our method differs in three
ways: (1) we use GRPO over the joint group y0 , y1 rather than AWR (advantage-weighted
regression, (Peng et al., 2019)); (2) our teacher conditions on a judge-generated reflection h
rather than raw feedback; and (3) unlike RLTF-SD, which targets tasks with verifiable rewards,
we focus on social simulation, where feedback is noisy, subjective, and multi-dimensional.
We compare against an RLTF-SD variant in our ablation study (section 6.2).

Connection to ERL Experiential RL (ERL) (Shi et al., 2026) instead uses supervised fine-
tuning (SFT) on reject-sampled teacher rollouts to internalize feedback into the student
model. In contrast, we train on all teacher rollouts with an RL objective, allowing us to
differentiate teacher rollout quality in continuous-reward settings and improve sample
efficiency. We also compare against an ERL variant in our ablation study.

Connection to SDPO Several works propose self-distillation policy optimization, distilling
textual feedback into the student with a reverse KL objective (Vapnik & Vashist, 2009; Vapnik
& Izmailov, 2015; Shenfeld et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2026; Hubotter et al., 2026). In contrast, our
approach uses a forward alternative: sampling from the teacher rather than the student,
and optimizing with group-normalized advantage. In addition, we optimize the feedback-
conditioned model via Lfb rather than using an EMA model. We also compare against
SDPO-like variants in our ablation study.

4     S OUL: Simulation Gym of Human-Like Behavior

resource covering the full breadth of human-like behavior. Existing benchmarks each focus
on a narrow slice, such as Theory of Mind, role-play, social interaction, or user simulation,
and use incompatible formats, metrics, and data splits (Yu et al., 2025; Du et al., 2026; Wang
et al., 2026; Wu et al., 2026). To address this, we introduce S OUL-Bench (Simulation gym Of
hUman-Like behavior), a unified benchmark and training suite spanning 10 tasks across six
categories of human simulation (Figure 3). For each task, S OUL provides a held-out test set

Theory of Mind. We include three tasks probing different aspects of mental-state reason-
ing. FanToM (Kim et al., 2023) tests ToM in multi-party conversations with information
asymmetry, requiring MCQ and open-ended reasoning about who knows what (1,086 test);
we select 18 conversations for testing and use the rest for training. HiToM (He et al., 2024)
tests higher-order nested beliefs with deception and private vs. public communication (100
test; MCQ); we use the official benchmark for evaluation and generate 200 additional stories
(6,000 training examples). ToMi (Nematzadeh et al., 2018) is a classic false-belief test; we use
the paraphrased ParaToMi version (Sclar et al., 2023) to test robust ToM reasoning beyond
surface cues (600 test; QA), with 6,004 paraphrased training examples.

Character Role Play. We include two character role-play tasks. CoSER (Wang et al., 2026)
evaluates literary character simulation from 771 books, where given a scenario, character
profile, and motivations, the model conducts a multi-turn dialogue (up to 20 turns) scored
by an LLM judge on Storyline Consistency, Anthropomorphism, Character Fidelity, and
Storyline Quality. We evaluate 200 conversations (100 ID, 100 OOD) and use the 26.5k-
dialogue training set. LifeChoices (Xu et al., 2024a) evaluates persona-driven decision
making with 1,462 decision points from 388 novels, where the model selects the option
most consistent with a character’s original decision given context, scenario, and identity,
emphasizing behavior-level fidelity and reasoning over characters’ knowledge, experiences,
and evolving motivations.

Social Skill. Sotopia (Zhou et al., 2024) evaluates social skills through dyadic agent
simulation. Given a scenario and private social goals for two agents, the agents interact
for up to 20 turns to achieve their goals. An LLM judge evaluates the interaction across
seven dimensions, including believability, relationship, knowledge, secret-keeping, financial
outcomes, and goal achievement. Following Zhou et al. (2026a), we evaluate on the Sotopia-
hard split of 100 scenarios. For training, we use the 2,310 scenarios from Sotopia-π (Yu et al.,
2025), derived from 462 base scenarios with 5 character-pair variations and no overlap with
the evaluation set.

faithfully simulate incorrect student reasoning in K-12 math. Given a multiple-choice math

User Simulation. MirrorBench (Hathidara et al., 2026) evaluates multi-turn human-like
user utterance generation: given a synthesized user goal, the model generates all user turns
while an LLM replays assistant responses, and the conversation is compared to the original
human dialogue using lexical diversity metrics (MATTR, HD-D, Yule’s K) and LLM-judge
realism scores. We evaluate 795 episodes across four domains and train on about 3,400
resampled conversations from the same corpora. UserLLM (Naous et al., 2025) evaluates
single-turn user utterance generation: given context and user intent, the model generates
one user message. We evaluate CSQA (role adherence), NQ (intent adherence), and PRISM
(diversity, intent decomposition, termination prediction, AI-detection; 1,800 test instances),
and train on datasets constructed from PRISM (intent consistency), NaturalQuestions (user
persistence), and CommonsenseQA (role fidelity).

Persona Simulation. TwinVoice (Du et al., 2025) evaluates persona simulation via a
discriminative task: given a user’s conversation history and a new anchor post, the model
selects the response that best matches the user’s communication style. The 5,687 evaluation
instances span social, interpersonal, and narrative persona, testing opinion consistency,
memory recall, and lexical fidelity, and we use accuracy as the metric. For training, we
construct 1,335 instances from three external corpora (Bluesky posts, Pushshift Telegram
messages, and Project Gutenberg novels); persona histories are generated from each source,
GPT-5.4-mini drafts candidate items, and GPT-5.4 filters them into the final multiple-choice
training set.

6     Experimental Results

6.1   Main Results

Table 1 presents results across S OUL tasks. We make the following observations.
(1) Compared with proprietary models and existing specialized models, our trained models
achieve clear improvements, demonstrating that our training environment S OUL is effective
for improving human-simulation capabilities. For example, both GRPO and D ITTO achieve
higher average scores than the strongest baseline, GPT-5.4. D ITTO outperforms GPT-5.4 on
6 out of 10 tasks and is slightly behind on the remaining 4 tasks. D ITTO also consistently
outperforms the previous best specialized human-simulation models on all tasks.
(2) Compared with GRPO, D ITTO achieves an average absolute improvement of 4.8% and
outperforms GRPO on 8 out of 10 datasets. This demonstrates the effectiveness of our
learning-from-verbal-feedback algorithm.
(3) Since our training uses GPT-5-nano as the judge model to provide feedback, we compare
against GPT-5-nano directly. Our model consistently outperforms GPT-5-nano on all tasks,
indicating that the improvements mainly come from utilizing judge feedback (e.g., multi-
dimensional evaluation and error analysis) rather than simply distilling a stronger model.
Task-wise, we find that D ITTO shows larger gains on more subjective tasks, where verbal
feedback provides richer guidance (e.g., Sotopia, UserLLM, LifeChoices, and Twinvoice).
In contrast, the gains are smaller on reasoning-heavy tasks such as Theory-of-Mind tasks.
This suggests that D ITTO is most effective for tasks with certain behavioral criteria (e.g.,
standards for achieving certain goals), rather than tasks dominated by logical reasoning.

Qualitative Feedback Analysis We further inspect the verbal feedback during training

same scenario, after the model adopts more structured negotiation strategies, the feedback

provides diagnostic supervision beyond scalar rewards by explaining how the response
should be improved.


7      Conclusion

We introduce D ITTO, a reinforcement learning approach that treats verbal feedback as a first-
class training signal for human behavior simulation. By generating feedback-conditioned
improved rollouts and jointly optimizing them with the original outputs, D ITTO enables the
base policy to internalize rich, actionable guidance without requiring feedback at test time.
To support this setting, we build S OUL, a unified training and evaluation suite spanning 10
tasks across six categories of human simulation. Experiments show that verbal feedback
substantially improves performance over standard GRPO, especially on generative and
multi-turn tasks, while also improving training efficiency and safety-related behaviors.

Limitations. Our approach has several limitations. First, we rely on LLM judges as
proxies for human judgment; while jury averaging reduces variance, it does not eliminate
systematic biases shared across all judges. Second, our evaluation is limited to English-
language benchmarks and may not generalize to other languages or cultural contexts.
Third, the training pipeline requires running K judges on N candidates per context, which
incurs significant computational cost. Finally, our theoretical variance-reduction guarantees
assume uncorrelated judge errors, which is an idealization—in practice, LLM judges may
share correlated failure modes.

C.3   Social Skill

Sotopia (Zhou et al., 2024) evaluates social interaction through two-agent simulations in
realistic scenarios. Each agent receives a character background, social goals, and relationship
context. Performance is measured across seven dimensions—believability, relationship,
knowledge, secret-keeping, social rules, financial outcomes, and goal achievement—scored
by LLM judges.

C.4   Learner Simulation

Mistakes (Ross & Andreas, 2025) evaluates whether LLMs can simulate incorrect student
reasoning in K-12 mathematics. Given a multiple-choice math problem and a misconception
description, models must select the wrong answer that corresponds to that specific miscon-
ception. This tests a key aspect of user simulation: faithfully reproducing systematic human
errors.

C.5   User Simulation

MirrorBench (Hathidara et al., 2026) evaluates user-proxy agents on producing human-
like utterances, decoupled from downstream task success. Given an extracted user goal,
the model iteratively generates all user turns in a conversation while assistant responses
are replayed from the original data. Each generated turn is compared against the real
human utterance. Evaluation uses both lexical diversity metrics (MATTR, Yule’s K, HD-D)
and LLM-judge metrics, calibrated against human-human and proxy-proxy baselines. We
evaluate on four subsets: ChatbotArena, ClariQ, OASST1, and QuLAC (see Section C.9 for
detailed dataset statistics and examples).

UserLLM (Naous et al., 2025) evaluates single-turn user utterance generation. Given a
conversation context and a user intent, the model generates one user turn. We evaluate
on three test sets, each targeting different metrics (see Section C.10 for detailed metric
definitions and examples):

      • CSQA (Role Adherence): The model plays a user who asks a commonsense ques-
        tion. Role adherence is measured by a substring-matching rule: the answer-choice
        texts are normalized and checked against the generated turn. If the turn contains


                                                      20
Reinforcing Human Behavior Simulation via Verbal Feedback




        one or two (but not all) of the answer choices, it scores 0—the model is leaking
        specific answers rather than staying in the questioner role. If it mentions none, it
        scores 1.
      • NQ (Intent Adherence): The model plays a user asking a factoid question. Intent
        adherence is measured by an LLM judge that classifies whether the generated turn
        refuses to answer the question (as a real user would) or inappropriately provides
        the answer itself.
      • PRISM (Multi-metric): The model generates first turns from high-level intents.
        Four metrics are computed: first-turn diversity (pairwise 1-gram Jaccard distance
        across generations), intent decomposition (1-gram overlap between intent keywords
        and the generated turn), termination F1 (whether the model correctly predicts con-
        versation ending), and AI detector score (whether the text passes an AI-detection
        classifier as human-written).

C.6   Persona Simulation

TwinVoice (Du et al., 2025) is a multi-dimensional benchmark for persona simulation
spanning Social Persona, Interpersonal Persona, and Narrative Persona. Given a persona
history and a new stimulus problem, the model must identify the response that best matches
the target persona in a discriminative 4-way multiple-choice task. The benchmark contains
5,687 test instances in total and reports accuracy, while also organizing examples around six
fine-grained capabilities, including opinion consistency, memory recall, logical reasoning,
lexical fidelity, persona tone, and syntactic style.

C.7   CoSER

CoSER (Wang et al., 2026) is a literary character role-playing benchmark built from 771 books
containing 17,966 characters. Using a “given-circumstance acting” (GCA) methodology
inspired by theater, models must simulate multi-turn dialogues as literary characters in
specified narrative settings. We describe the generation protocol, evaluation metrics, and
dataset details below.

Generation protocol. Each task (called a circumstance) specifies a scene from a book,
including: (1) a scenario describing the setting, atmosphere, and dramatic tension; (2) a
plot summary providing narrative context; (3) detailed character profiles for each speaking
character; and (4) character motivations (inner thoughts and goals). The model must
simulate a multi-character conversation in this setting.
The actor model under evaluation plays all characters in the conversation—the same LLM
sequentially portrays each character c ∈ C in the given setting. Two auxiliary agents support
the simulation:

• Character agents (actor model): One instantiation per speaking character. Each receives
  the scenario, its own profile and motivation, and the profiles of other characters. It
  generates dialogue in the format [inner thought] speech (action).
• Environment agent (auxiliary): Describes environmental changes and minor character
  reactions based on the ongoing dialogue (e.g., crowd murmurs, weather shifts).
• Next-speaker predictor (NSP) (auxiliary): After each turn, predicts which character
  speaks next (or outputs <END CHAT> to terminate). Termination requires a minimum of 5
  rounds.

Conversations run for up to 20 rounds. In practice, the NSP terminates most conversations
earlier (mean: 14.2 character turns, range: 6–20).
The generated conversation is then compared against the ground-truth dialogue from the
original book, which serves as the reference for evaluation.

Evaluation protocol. Evaluation combines LLM-based flaw detection with automatic
lexical metrics. For each of four dimensions, an LLM judge (acting as a “literary critic”)


                                             21
Reinforcing Human Behavior Simulation via Verbal Feedback




receives the full context—book title, plot summary, scenario, character profiles, the reference
conversation, and the dimension-specific evaluation criteria—and identifies specific flaws in
the simulated conversation. Each flaw is assigned a severity from 1 (minor) to 5 (severe).
The per-dimension score is computed as:
                                                                            
          score = max 0, min 100 − (total severity − 0.3 × Nturns ) × 5, 100
where Nturns is the number of character turns in the simulation. The 0.3 × Nturns term
provides a length-based tolerance: longer conversations are expected to accumulate more
minor flaws. The overall reward is the mean of four dimension scores, normalized to [0, 1].
Additionally, BLEU and ROUGE-L are computed against the reference dialogue, though
these are typically low (BLEU ≈ 0.02) since valid simulations can differ substantially in
wording from the original.

Evaluation dimensions. The four dimensions capture different facets of role-playing
quality:

• Storyline Consistency: Whether characters’ reactions (emotions, attitudes, behaviors)
  align with those in the reference conversation. Detects deviations from the original
  dramatic arc.
• Anthropomorphism: How human-like and natural characters behave. Checks for: self-
  identity (goals, decisions, preferences vs. “helpful AI assistant” behavior), emotional
  depth (psychological complexity, use of subtext), persona coherence (consistent personal-
  ity), and social interaction (understanding of others, contextual reactions).
• Character Fidelity: How well characters match their established profiles from the book.
  Evaluated only for major characters. Checks: appropriate language/vocabulary for the
  character’s background, character-specific knowledge, personality-consistent emotions
  and behaviors, and appropriate social dynamics given relationships and status.
• Storyline Quality: Logical consistency and narrative quality. Checks: natural flow and
  progression (no verbosity, redundancy, or repetition), and factual consistency between
  statements.

Human validation. The original paper validates the LLM-based evaluation against human
judgments. Three annotators scored 7 representative LLMs on 60 samples using a 1–10
scale. Human rankings generally align with LLM-judged scores: CoSER-70B ranks highest
under both human (6.78 avg, 86.9% win rate) and LLM evaluation. The full GCA evaluation
protocol achieves 68.6% alignment with human judges (GPT-4o as critic); DeepSeek-R1 as
critic achieves 77.5%. Ablation analysis shows all GCA components contribute: removing
reference dialogues, scoring rubrics, length correction, or dimension separation each reduces
alignment. Interestingly, simple n-gram metrics (BLEU: 75.3%, ROUGE-L: 72.0%) achieve
competitive alignment with LLM judges, though they cannot capture the nuanced flaw-level
feedback that GCA provides.

Metrics. MirrorBench combines two metric families: three lexical diversity metrics and
three judge-based realism metrics, capturing complementary facets of human-likeness.

Lexical diversity metrics measure vocabulary richness and repetition patterns of the proxy’s
user turns, z-score normalized against the human distribution on the same dataset:
• MATTR (Moving-Average Type-Token Ratio): vocabulary diversity over a sliding win-
  dow (w = 50).
• HD-D (Hypergeometric Distribution Diversity): vocabulary diversity robust to sequence
  length.
• Yule’s K: repetitiveness from the token-frequency spectrum (higher K = more repetition).

Judge-based realism metrics use LLM judges to assess whether the proxy’s user turns sound
like a real human. The three metrics differ in what information the judge receives and what
question it answers:
• GTEval (reference-comparative): The judge sees both the proxy conversation and the
  human reference side by side, and scores their similarity on [0, 1]. This measures how
  closely the proxy reproduces the specific human behavior.
• Pairwise Indistinguishability (PI) (reference-discriminative): The judge sees two
  anonymized conversations—one proxy, one human—in random order (labeled A/B)
  and picks which user sounds more human. This is a blind A/B test; the score is the
  proxy’s win rate. A score near 0.5 means the proxy is indistinguishable from the human.
• Rubric-and-Reason (RNR) (reference-free): The judge sees only the proxy conversation
  and rates whether the user sounds human-like based on a rubric covering style, behavior,
  and tone. Returns YES/NO. This judges realism in absolute terms without any human
  reference.

Each judge metric is executed with a self-consistency parameter c ≥ 1 (repeated judgments
under the same prompt). Scores are calibrated via Human–Human (HH) and Proxy–Proxy
(PP) controls: HH compares the human conversation to itself (expected ceiling ≈ 1.0 for
GTEval, ≈ 0.5 for PI), and PP compares the proxy to itself (expected baseline).


Our adaptation. In our evaluation framework, we run MirrorBench in single-turn assistant
mode: the user-proxy LLM generates all user turns iteratively, but assistant responses are
replayed verbatim from the original dataset rather than generated by a separate LLM. This
simplification is inconsequential for conversations with a single user turn (most of Arena,
all of QuLAC), since there is no subsequent turn where assistant coherence matters. For
multi-turn conversations (all of ClariQ, most of OASST1), replayed assistant responses
may become contextually misaligned if the proxy’s user turn diverges from the original,
potentially causing cascading drift. We use GPT-5.4 as the judge model for all metrics.

C.10   UserLLM

UserLLM (Naous et al., 2025) evaluates single-turn user utterance generation: given a
conversation context and a user intent, the model generates one user turn. We describe the
generation protocol, evaluation metrics, and dataset details below.

Generation protocol. Each task provides an intent (a natural-language description of the
user’s goal) and a conversation history (prior user–assistant turns). The model receives a
system prompt instructing it to act as a realistic human user—including occasional typos,
imperfect punctuation, and information splitting across turns—and generates the next user
message. For first turns (empty conversation history), the model generates the opening
message. For sequential turns, the model also receives a termination instruction: it may
append a special token <|endconversation|> to signal that the conversation should end.

Evaluation granularity. Each task is scored independently. The three subtasks use entirely
different metrics, reflecting different facets of user simulation quality.

Metrics.    UserLLM defines three subtasks, each with its own metric:

CSQA — Role Adherence tests whether the model stays in the questioner role rather than
leaking answers. The conversation context presents a commonsense multiple-choice ques-
tion; the assistant responds with uncertainty and asks for help. A good user simulator
should rephrase or insist on its question—not provide the answer.

Among the RL-based methods that use verbal feedback, RLTF-SD and our approach share
the same self-distillation structure—both generate a teacher rollout conditioned on feedback
and train the base policy to internalize the teacher’s improvements. The key algorithmic dis-
tinction is the optimization method: RLTF-SD uses advantage-weighted regression (AWR),
which performs weighted supervised learning on teacher outputs without importance cor-
rection, while our method uses GRPO over the joint student-teacher group with trust-region
clipping. Experiential RL also follows a reflect-and-revise pattern but consolidates improve-
ments through supervised fine-tuning on successful second attempts rather than through
on-policy RL.
